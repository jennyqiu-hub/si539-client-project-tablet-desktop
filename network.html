Networking & Building Connections

Build Connections Without Feeling “Salesy”

Networking is simply learning from people and building relationships over time. You do not need a perfect pitch or an outgoing personality. A clear goal, a short message, and consistent follow-through are enough to get started.

If you want feedback on your outreach approach, you can talk to a CDO Career Coach .

Start Here

  1. Pick one direction: choose an industry, role, or company type you want to explore.
  2. Find 5 people: alumni, classmates, friends of friends, or professionals with similar interests.
  3. Send a short outreach message: ask for 10–15 minutes to learn, not to request a job.
  4. Prepare 6–8 questions: focus on the person’s work, choices, and advice.
  5. Follow up: thank them, summarize one takeaway, and keep the connection warm.

Conducting an Informational Interview

Before the Conversation

  • Do basic research: read their LinkedIn, recent work, and the organization’s mission or products.
  • Set a simple goal: learn about the role, confirm fit, or understand how to break in.
  • Bring your story in one sentence: who you are + what you’re exploring + why you reached out.

During the Conversation

  • Open with gratitude and time: confirm you’ll keep it to 10–15 minutes.
  • Ask and listen: let them speak; take brief notes.
  • Close with a next step: “Is there anyone else you recommend I talk to?”

After the Conversation

  • Send a thank-you: within 24 hours, include one specific takeaway.
  • Track what you learned: role insights, keywords, tools, and next actions.
  • Maintain the relationship: a short update after a few weeks is enough.

Outreach Messages

Keep outreach short and respectful. Your goal is a small learning conversation, not a job request.

Simple Outreach Template

Subject: Quick question from a UMSI student

Message:

Hi [Name],

I’m a UMSI student exploring [field/role]. I saw your work in [area/company] and would love to learn how you got started.

Would you be open to a 10–15 minute chat sometime this week or next? I’m happy to work around your schedule.

Thank you,
[Your Name]

Follow-Up Template

Hi [Name],

Thanks again for your time—your point about [specific takeaway] was especially helpful. I’m going to [action you will take].

If it’s helpful, I’d love to stay in touch and share a quick update after I’ve made progress.

Best,
[Your Name]

Questions You Can Ask

Choose questions that reveal how the person thinks and how the role works day-to-day. Aim for 6–8 questions.

  • Role clarity: What does a typical week look like for you?
  • Skills: Which skills matter most in your role, and how did you build them?
  • Hiring signals: What makes candidates stand out for entry-level roles?
  • Path: What was your path into this work, and what would you do differently?
  • Tools: What tools or methods do you use most often?
  • Culture: How does your team collaborate and measure success?
  • Advice: If I want to move toward this role, what should I do in the next 30 days?
  • Referrals: Is there anyone else you recommend I speak with?
